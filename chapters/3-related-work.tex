\chapter{Methodology: Temporal Alignment and Refinement}
\label{c:methodology}

The foundation models introduced in Chapter~\ref{c:background} reconstruct each frame
independently. Applied to a video sequence, each per-frame reconstruction lands in its own
arbitrary coordinate system, with no correspondence between the scale, rotation, or
translation of one frame and the next. Concatenating these reconstructions directly produces
an incoherent four-dimensional point cloud, even when the underlying per-frame geometry is
accurate. Two complementary contributions address this problem. Section~\ref{sec:alignment_strategies}
presents three temporal alignment strategies that bring independently reconstructed frames
into a shared coordinate system, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:strategies}.
Section~\ref{sec:opt} introduces Confidence-Weighted Temporal Smoothing (Opt), a
refinement step that suppresses residual frame-to-frame jitter in static scene regions
without altering dynamic content.

\section{Temporal alignment strategies}
\label{sec:alignment_strategies}

Independently reconstructed frames each live in their own arbitrary coordinate system.
Producing a coherent 4D point cloud therefore requires estimating a similarity
transformation for each frame and composing all frames into a single shared system.
Each alignment strategy presented here relies on Umeyama similarity alignment
(Section~\ref{sec:math_background}), which estimates the scale, rotation, and translation
that best register one point set to another. The three strategies differ only in which
pairs of frames are aligned and in what order. All three operate exclusively on static
scene correspondences, since moving content would otherwise corrupt the alignment solver
with spurious matches.

\subsection{Static mask derivation}
\label{sec:static_mask_derivation}

Identifying static correspondences requires distinguishing static from dynamic scene
regions in every frame. Figure~\ref{fig:static_mask_derivation} illustrates the two-stage
process used to produce these masks. A motion prior is first computed from frame-wise
pixel differences between consecutive frames, flagging regions that change substantially
over time. Because this signal is noisy and often fails to capture the full extent of a
moving subject, particularly for textureless surfaces such as skin or clothing, the prior
is refined using SAM2~\cite{sam2} (Section~\ref{sec:sam2_background}). SAM2 propagates
the motion prior into a complete segmentation mask that covers the full moving subject,
including low-texture regions that the raw difference signal alone would miss. The
resulting per-frame mask designates each pixel as static or dynamic, and serves as the
basis for all three alignment strategies.

On Hi4D, the background is removed during preprocessing so that only the human subjects
are visible to the models; the static mask derivation is therefore not applied, and the
alignment strategies proceed without static-scene correspondences. Results on Hi4D should
be interpreted with this limitation in mind, as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:datasets}.

\subsection{Strategy overview}
\label{sec:strategy_overview}

Figure~\ref{fig:strategies} illustrates the three strategies for a sequence of $T$ frames
indexed $t_0, \dots, t_F$. Strategy 1 aligns every frame directly to a single reference
frame. Strategy 2 aligns frames pairwise in a hierarchical merge. Strategy 3 estimates all
pairwise alignments and combines them through global optimisation. Each strategy trades off
computational cost against robustness to unreliable individual alignments, as detailed
below.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/strategies_figure_cropped}
\caption{The three temporal alignment strategies. (a) Strategy 1: Reference. Every frame
$t_i$ is aligned directly to the reference frame $t_0$ using static correspondences.
(b) Strategy 2: Hierarchical. Frames are merged pairwise in a binary tree, with each
level combining adjacent pairs until all frames share a single coordinate system.
(c) Strategy 3: Pose-Graph. All $\mathcal{O}(T^2)$ pairwise alignments are estimated and
combined through iterative geodesic averaging; edge thickness indicates alignment
reliability, with high-weight edges (thick blue) contributing more to the global solution
than low-weight edges (thin blue).}
\label{fig:strategies}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Strategy 1: Reference}
\label{sec:strategy1}

The reference strategy designates the first frame $t_0$ as a fixed anchor. For every
subsequent frame $t_i$, a similarity transformation is estimated directly between the
static correspondences of $t_i$ and $t_0$, and applied to bring $t_i$ into the coordinate
system of $t_0$. The reference strategy requires only $T - 1$ pairwise alignments, making
it the simplest and fastest of the three. Its main limitation is sensitivity to the
reference frame: if $t_0$ is poorly observed or the subject moves far from its initial
pose, alignment accuracy degrades for frames temporally distant from $t_0$.

\subsubsection{Strategy 2: Hierarchical}
\label{sec:strategy2}

The hierarchical strategy avoids dependence on a single reference frame by building a
binary merge tree over the sequence. Frames are first paired and aligned to one another;
each resulting pair is then aligned to a neighbouring pair, and the process repeats until
all frames share a common coordinate system. Because each merge operates on a smaller
temporal offset than a direct alignment to $t_0$, individual alignment steps are generally
more reliable. The strategy requires $\mathcal{O}(T)$ total alignments distributed across
$\log_2 T$ tree levels.

\subsubsection{Strategy 3: Pose-graph optimisation}
\label{sec:strategy3}

Hierarchical alignment propagates errors along a single tree path: a poor alignment at one
level corrupts all frames downstream. The pose-graph strategy addresses this by estimating
a similarity transformation for every pair of frames, producing $\mathcal{O}(T^2)$
pairwise edges. Each edge is assigned a weight inversely proportional to its Umeyama
alignment residual,
\begin{equation}
    w_{ij} = \frac{1}{\varepsilon_{ij}},
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon_{ij}$ denotes the residual alignment error between frames $i$ and $j$.
Edges with lower residual error receive higher weight, giving more reliable pairwise
alignments greater influence on the final solution.

Starting from the Strategy 1 alignment as an initial guess, the optimisation iteratively
refines every frame's pose by aggregating proposals from all connected neighbours. For
frame $i$, each neighbour $j$ proposes a pose by composing its current pose with the
corresponding edge transformation. Scales are averaged in log-space to respect their
multiplicative structure, translations use weighted Euclidean averaging, and rotations are
combined using the Weiszfeld algorithm for geodesic L1 averaging on $\mathrm{SO}(3)$
(Section~\ref{sec:math_background}). Robust rotation averaging allows the pose-graph
strategy to down-weight outlying pairwise alignments rather than letting them corrupt the
global solution. The procedure is run for a fixed number of iterations (default 50).

By propagating information across many pairwise edges rather than a single path, the
pose-graph strategy is the most robust of the three to individually unreliable alignments.
The robustness comes at the cost of $\mathcal{O}(T^2)$ pairwise alignment computations,
making it the slowest of the three strategies.

\subsection{Sequence-level alignment}
\label{sec:sequence_alignment}

After any of the three strategies brings all frames into a shared coordinate system, the
resulting per-frame point clouds are concatenated, as are the corresponding ground-truth
frames. A final Umeyama alignment registers the concatenated reconstruction to the
concatenated ground truth, producing the sequence-level metrics reported in
Chapter~\ref{c:results}.

\section{Confidence-weighted temporal smoothing (Opt)}
\label{sec:opt}

Temporal alignment produces a geometrically coherent sequence, but residual per-frame
reconstruction noise still causes static scene points to drift slightly from one frame to
the next. Opt is a lightweight, training-free post-processing step that suppresses this
residual jitter directly, without modifying dynamic content. Opt operates on the output of
Strategy 2, chosen as the alignment basis because it produces the most stable
sequence-level geometry among the three strategies evaluated in Chapter~\ref{c:results}.

\subsection{Method}
\label{sec:opt_method}

Once all frames are expressed in a shared coordinate system, points belonging to static
scene regions should remain stable over time, since the underlying surface does not move.
Any frame-to-frame variation in static regions is therefore attributable to reconstruction
noise rather than genuine scene motion, and can be suppressed without discarding real
information.

Opt applies a Gaussian filter along the temporal dimension to the aligned, dense per-view
pointmaps. Let $\mathbf{p}_{t,v,h,w} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ denote the 3D point at pixel
$(v, h, w)$ in frame $t$, and let $c_{t,v,h,w} \in \mathbb{R}^+$ denote the model
confidence at that pixel. For each static pixel, the combined weight from neighbouring
frame $t'$ is
\begin{equation}
    W(t', t, v, h, w) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{(t' - t)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
    \cdot\, c_{t',v,h,w}
    \cdot\, \mathbf{1}_{\mathrm{static}}(t', v, h, w),
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the temporal window and $\mathbf{1}_{\mathrm{static}}$ is the SAM2-derived
static mask. The smoothed position is the confidence-weighted temporal mean,
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{p}}_{t,v,h,w}
    = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{t'} W(t', t, v, h, w)\;\mathbf{p}_{t',v,h,w}}
           {\displaystyle\sum_{t'} W(t', t, v, h, w)},
\end{equation}
and the final output blends the smoothed and original estimates,
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\mathbf{p}}_{t,v,h,w}
    = (1 - \alpha)\,\mathbf{p}_{t,v,h,w}
    + \alpha\,\hat{\mathbf{p}}_{t,v,h,w},
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \in [0, 1]$ is the blending strength. Dynamic pixels are left entirely
untouched, preventing the filter from suppressing genuine object or human motion. Because
Opt relies only on model confidence, temporal proximity, and the static mask, with no
reference to ground-truth geometry, it is applicable at inference time without access to
evaluation data.

\subsection{Parameter selection}
\label{sec:opt_parameters}

The optimal values of $\sigma$ and $\alpha$ depend on the baseline jitter of the
underlying backbone: a backbone with higher per-frame instability benefits from a wider
temporal window. Parameters are selected per backbone by sweeping
$\sigma \in \{0.5, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 12, 20, 30\}$ and $\alpha \in \{0.2, 0.5, 0.8, 1.0\}$,
and measuring the resulting trade-off between jitter reduction and geometric fidelity
relative to the unsmoothed Strategy 2 output. The sweep traces a Pareto frontier between
temporal stability and reconstruction accuracy, reported in full in Chapter~\ref{c:results},
from which a single operating point is selected per backbone.

The selected configurations are $\sigma = 6$, $\alpha = 1.0$ for MASt3R-SfM;
$\sigma = 4$, $\alpha = 1.0$ for PI3 and PI3X; and $\sigma = 2$, $\alpha = 1.0$ for VGGT.
MASt3R-SfM requires the widest temporal window, consistent with it exhibiting the highest
baseline jitter of the four backbones at every camera count evaluated.

These per-backbone configurations represent a single operating point on the Pareto frontier
and do not account for variation within a backbone. Individual subjects within a dataset
exhibit at least two distinct jitter profiles that would each benefit from different
$\sigma$ and $\alpha$ values, so a globally fixed configuration is necessarily a
compromise. Selecting $\sigma$ and $\alpha$ at run time, or calibrating them on a broader
dataset, would likely improve on the configurations reported here; this direction is
discussed further in Chapter~\ref{c:discussion}.
