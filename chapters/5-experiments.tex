\chapter{Results}
\label{c:results}

This chapter presents the experimental results across all model families, alignment
strategies, and view counts evaluated on DexYCB and Hi4D. Section~\ref{sec:3d_baseline}
establishes the per-frame geometric performance ceiling before any temporal alignment.
Section~\ref{sec:alignment_comparison} compares the three temporal alignment strategies.
Section~\ref{sec:sparse_view} analyses sensitivity to camera count.
Section~\ref{sec:native4d_baselines} evaluates native 4D architectures and geometry
refinement. Section~\ref{sec:opt_results} presents the Opt smoothing results.
Section~\ref{sec:qualitative} provides qualitative point cloud analysis.

\section{3D Baseline Results}
\label{sec:3d_baseline}

Table~\ref{tab:baseline_control_ggpt} reports per-frame geometric performance 
for all six models and GGPT on DexYCB and Hi4D across two, three, and four 
input views. Spatial models consistently outperform native 4D architectures at 
every view count and on both datasets; GGPT degrades geometry without exception. 
These per-frame aligned results serve as the geometric ceiling: no temporal 
alignment or smoothing has been applied, and each frame is registered 
independently to its ground-truth counterpart.

On DexYCB, Pi3 achieves the best Chamfer distance at two and four views 
(0.018\,m and 0.019\,m respectively), with static accuracy exceeding 91\,\% 
at both. Pi3X leads on dynamic accuracy across most view counts, reaching 
78.6\,\% at two views and 89.7\,\% at four views --- the highest dynamic 
accuracy among all spatial models. MASt3R-SfM achieves the best Chamfer at 
three views (0.020\,m), while VGGT improves monotonically from 0.027\,m to 
0.024\,m across view counts with static accuracy reaching 84.0\,\% at four 
views. The four spatial models consistently outperform both native 4D 
architectures: VGGT4D reaches 0.049\,m at two views and degrades to 0.069\,m 
at four, while MonoFusion reaches 0.080\,m at two views and 0.036\,m at four, 
with static accuracy remaining below 51\,\% at every configuration.

On Hi4D, the model ordering is consistent with DexYCB. Pi3 achieves the lowest 
Chamfer at every view count (0.079\,m, 0.070\,m, 0.067\,m), and the highest 
dynamic accuracy and completeness across most configurations. Pi3X follows 
closely (0.081\,m, 0.074\,m, 0.079\,m). Dynamic accuracy values on Hi4D are 
substantially lower than on DexYCB across all models, reflecting the greater 
difficulty of close human--human interaction sequences with stronger 
self-occlusions and larger non-rigid motion. VGGT4D and MonoFusion degrade more 
severely than spatial models on this out-of-distribution dataset, with VGGT4D 
reaching 0.267\,m at three views and MonoFusion 0.406\,m at four views. Static 
accuracy and completeness are not reported for Hi4D, since the background is 
masked during preprocessing and all evaluated points belong to dynamic subject 
regions (Section~\ref{sec:datasets}).

GGPT worsens Chamfer for every backbone without exception, a result discussed 
in detail in Section~\ref{sec:native4d_baselines}

The central finding is that strategy choice has no meaningful effect on 
downstream performance. On DexYCB, all three strategies produce Chamfer 
distances that differ by at most 0.001\,m across every model, and 
$\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$ differences lie within one standard deviation 
throughout. Camera pose metrics follow the same pattern: ATE and RPE vary by 
less than 0.01\,m across strategies for every backbone. Strategy 2 is selected 
as the alignment basis for the temporal smoothing refinement in 
Section~\ref{sec:opt_results}, with the rationale discussed in 
Chapter~\ref{c:discussion}; the results here confirm that this choice does not 
meaningfully affect the conclusions.

On Hi4D, the strategies were designed to exploit static correspondences as 
stable anchors for temporal registration, which are absent after preprocessing 
(Section~\ref{sec:datasets}). For Pi3 and Pi3X, all three strategies produce 
identical results across every metric. For MASt3R-SfM and VGGT, small 
differences appear, but these lie within overlapping standard deviations and 
reflect the application to all points instead of only dynamic points, rather than meaningful strategy 
differences. Hi4D results are therefore reported for completeness and reflect 
the performance of the underlying models rather than properties of the 
alignment strategies.

\section{Sparse-View Sensitivity}
\label{sec:sparse_view}

The camera configurations of Section~\ref{sec:datasets} span two, three, and
four views, progressively increasing multi-view coverage. This section isolates
the effect of camera count on reconstruction quality across all six models,
drawing on both the per-frame baseline results of
Table~\ref{tab:baseline_control_ggpt} and the Strategy 2 aligned outputs of
Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}, which reports the full
per-view-count breakdown on DexYCB covering geometric fidelity,
static/dynamic decomposition, and temporal stability at each level of
sparsity. Opt and GGPT results are included in
Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb} for reference; their detailed
analysis follows in Sections~\ref{sec:native4d_baselines}
and~\ref{sec:opt_results}. For spatial models, additional views translate
reliably into better reconstruction. For VGGT4D and MonoFusion, the
relationship is more complex, and the two models fail in qualitatively
different ways.

Spatial models improve reliably with additional views and remain close to
their per-frame geometric ceiling reported in
Table~\ref{tab:baseline_control_ggpt}. Pi3X shows the strongest overall
gains: dynamic accuracy rises from 59.3\,\% at two views to 88.5\,\% at
four views, and static accuracy reaches 94.3\,\% at four views, the highest
value across all models and view counts. Pi3 improves similarly, with Chamfer
decreasing from 0.031\,m to 0.026\,m and static accuracy rising from 71.2\,\%
to 87.0\,\%. MASt3R-SfM benefits most from the addition of a third camera,
where Chamfer drops sharply from 0.043\,m to 0.023\,m, approaching its
per-frame ceiling of 0.020\,m. VGGT improves monotonically from 0.034\,m
to 0.027\,m in Chamfer and from 64.2\,\% to 74.1\,\% in static accuracy.

VGGT4D responds to additional views non-monotonically. At the per-frame
level (Table~\ref{tab:baseline_control_ggpt}), Chamfer improves from
0.049\,m at two views to 0.040\,m at three views, suggesting that the
additional camera provides useful geometric information. After temporal
alignment (Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}), however, Chamfer
rises sharply to 0.069\,m at four views, worse than the two-view result
and worse than every spatial model at every view count. Static completeness
follows the same pattern, dropping from 52.9\,\% at three views to 30.5\,\%
at four views, while static accuracy increases monotonically to 69.1\,\%,
indicating that the surviving reconstructed points are individually
well-placed but that a large portion of the surface is lost. As documented
in Section~\ref{sec:qualitative}, this pattern is consistent with the joint
transformer averaging conflicting depth hypotheses from the additional camera
rather than resolving them, producing the characteristic spatial smearing
visible in Figure~\ref{fig:vggt4d_smearing}.

MonoFusion presents a different picture. Chamfer improves from 0.081\,m at
two views to 0.031\,m at both three and four views, and this improvement is
consistent at the per-frame level as well (Table~\ref{tab:baseline_control_ggpt}).
This improvement does not translate to precision at the 1\,cm threshold,
however: static accuracy reaches only 54.4\,\% and 62.0\,\% at three and
four views, compared to 89.7\,\% and 87.0\,\% for Pi3 at the same view
counts. At two views, static accuracy drops to 15.5\,\% with static
completeness at 19.9\,\%, consistent with the duplicated-subject artefacts
documented in Section~\ref{sec:qualitative}.

\section{Native 4D Architectures and Geometry Refinement}
\label{sec:native4d_baselines}

\paragraph{VGGT4D.}
The per-frame results of Section~\ref{sec:3d_baseline} establish that VGGT4D's
geometric fidelity is substantially weaker than every spatial model before any
temporal considerations enter the picture. This gap is architectural: joint
sequence-level processing couples temporal consistency into the reconstruction
loop itself, preventing the per-frame estimator from maximising geometric fidelity
independently. To isolate this cost, VGGT4D's native 4D output is aligned
independently to ground truth frame by frame---the same per-frame registration
applied to spatial baselines---providing a best-case geometric measure that removes
any advantage or disadvantage from temporal consistency.

Even under this best-case alignment, the gap persists when spatial models are
lifted to 4D via the temporal alignment strategies of Chapter~\ref{c:methodology}.
Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb} shows that at four views, VGGT4D's
Chamfer rises to 0.069\,m---higher than its own two-view result---while Pi3
Strategy 2 achieves 0.026\,m and static accuracy of 87.0\,\% against VGGT4D's
69.1\,\%. The non-monotonic behaviour across view counts, where performance
improves from two to three views but degrades sharply at four, is documented
qualitatively in Section~\ref{sec:qualitative}. VGGT4D does reduce jitter
relative to unsmoothed spatial models (0.003\,m mean at two and three views),
but Section~\ref{sec:opt_results} shows that spatial models with Opt smoothing
match or improve on this without the geometric cost.

\paragraph{MonoFusion.}
MonoFusion represents the opposite trade-off: it achieves the lowest jitter of
all evaluated methods at every view count (0.001\,m mean,
Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}), but at the cost of the weakest
geometry among all models. The 2-view failure is qualitatively distinct from
VGGT4D's smearing---MonoFusion produces duplicated-subject artefacts rather than
a degraded single reconstruction, as documented in Section~\ref{sec:qualitative}.
These artefacts arise from the local-optima susceptibility of MonoFusion's
iterative depth alignment under insufficient multi-view overlap
(Section~\ref{sec:temporal_models}), and render its output unreliable as a
refinement input, which is why GGPT is not applied to MonoFusion on Hi4D.

\paragraph{GGPT.}
GGPT consistently worsens Chamfer distance across every backbone and both
datasets without exception, and is therefore unsuitable for geometry-critical
applications in the sparse-view dynamic setting evaluated here. The negative
$\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$ values for GGPT on Hi4D in
Table~\ref{tab:strategy_extended} do not indicate genuine geometric improvement:
GGPT's geometry-destructive effect is so severe that the per-frame baseline is
already substantially worse than the aligned 4D output, making temporal
aggregation appear to reduce Chamfer relative to that degraded starting point.
The full per-backbone and per-dataset degradation factors are reported in
Section~\ref{sec:3d_baseline}.


\section{Temporal smoothing refinement}
\label{sec:opt_results}
As established in Section~\ref{sec:temporal_models}, per-frame reconstruction
produces frame-to-frame variation in static scene regions that is purely an
artefact of reconstruction noise rather than genuine scene motion. The temporal
alignment strategies of Chapter~\ref{c:methodology} lift these per-frame
reconstructions into a common coordinate system, producing a 4D representation,
but do not address the underlying per-pixel depth instability that causes jitter,
since each frame remains independently reconstructed. This residual instability
is the target of the confidence-weighted temporal smoothing method (Opt), whose
effect on reconstruction quality and temporal consistency is evaluated in the
following results. Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} situates all evaluated methods in the
geometry--jitter trade-off space. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline_progression} traces
the effect of each pipeline stage on both jitter and Chamfer at two views, with
native 4D architectures and GGPT shown as reference baselines. Numerical detail
is drawn from Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}.

\FloatBarrier

\subsection{High-frequency Jitter Elimination}
HF jitter is defined in Section~\ref{sec:metrics} as second-order
temporal acceleration, sensitive to rapid oscillations rather than
slow drift.
The reduction in HF jitter is the strongest result in
Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}.
Across every model and every view count, Opt reduces HF jitter to a
value that rounds to 0.000\,m in Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb},
compared to baseline Strategy-2 values ranging from 0.008\,m to
0.019\,m depending on model and view count.
The native 4D architecture VGGT4D achieves only 0.005\,m at two views.
This result follows directly from the filter design: as the Gaussian temporal filter
defined in Section~\ref{sec:opt_method} acts as a low-pass filter over
the temporal frequency domain, it attenuates high-frequency oscillations
by construction, and the second-order finite difference used to measure
HF jitter is precisely the signal it suppresses.

\subsection{Mean Jitter Reduction}
Mean jitter captures lower-frequency instability that the Gaussian
attenuates less completely than high-frequency oscillations.
Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline_progression} shows a consistent reduction
across all four spatial models.
At two views, MASt3R-SfM mean jitter falls from 0.011\,m
(Strategy 2) to 0.001\,m (Opt), a 91\,\% reduction consistent with
its wide temporal window ($\sigma = 6$).
VGGT falls from 0.009\,m to 0.003\,m (67\,\%), Pi3 from 0.006\,m to
0.002\,m (67\,\%), and Pi3X from 0.005\,m to 0.002\,m (60\,\%).
The reductions are consistent across three and four views.
MASt3R-SfM and VGGT reach 0.001\,m mean jitter at every view count,
approaching MonoFusion's 0.001\,m level.
All four Opt-smoothed models cross below the VGGT4D mean jitter
reference line of 0.003\,m visible in
Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline_progression}.


\subsection{Drift and the Limits of Temporal Smoothing}
Drift measures a qualitatively different failure mode from jitter.
Whereas jitter is high-frequency noise, drift is a low-frequency bias:
a slow accumulation of pose estimation errors that displaces static
anchors monotonically over the sequence. Because the Gaussian temporal
filter defined in Section~\ref{sec:opt_method} is a low-pass filter,
it is by design insensitive to this frequency range, and no reduction
in drift should be expected from Opt. Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}
confirms this: Opt does not reliably reduce drift, and in some cases
increases it. Where drift increases under Opt, the temporal averaging
spreads the low-frequency alignment bias across the smoothing window
rather than correcting it, since the underlying coordinate system errors
that cause drift are not addressed by the filter.
MonoFusion achieves consistently near-zero drift (0.001\,m at every
view count).
GGPT\,(S2) reduces drift relative to Strategy 2 in several cases,
but not without substantially degrading Chamfer distance, and the
improvement is not consistent across view counts or backbones.

\subsection{Geometric Cost}
The right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline_progression} shows that the Chamfer
distance of three of the four models remains near or below the Strategy 2 level after
Opt smoothing. For MASt3R-SfM, Chamfer improves slightly from 0.023\,m to 0.022\,m
at three views and is unchanged at 0.026\,m at four views. For VGGT, Chamfer is
unchanged at 0.028\,m at three views and increases marginally from 0.027\,m to
0.029\,m at four views. For Pi3, Chamfer improves at two views from 0.031\,m to
0.026\,m and remains competitive at three and four views. Pi3X is the exception: Opt
increases Chamfer from 0.027\,m to 0.040\,m at two views (+48\,\%) and from 0.032\,m
to 0.035--0.036\,m at three and four views (+9--13\,\%).

The full per-view breakdown in Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb} additionally
reveals the effect on static accuracy and completeness, showing whether the temporal
averaging bias introduced by Opt acts as a corrective or a degrading influence on
static reconstruction quality. For MASt3R-SfM and Pi3, the bias is corrective: at
three and four views, static accuracy and completeness both improve under Opt
(MASt3R-SfM: 73.8\,\%\,$\to$\,75.5\,\% accuracy and 66.3\,\%\,$\to$\,68.3\,\%
completeness at three views; Pi3: 71.2\,\%\,$\to$\,76.7\,\% accuracy and
62.2\,\%\,$\to$\,67.5\,\% completeness at two views), indicating that the temporal
mean pulls noisy static point estimates closer to their true positions. For VGGT, the
effect is approximately neutral, with static accuracy and completeness changing by less
than one percentage point across view counts. For Pi3X, both static accuracy and
completeness degrade consistently (e.g., 90.9\,\%\,$\to$\,85.2\,\% accuracy and
75.7\,\%\,$\to$\,72.1\,\% completeness at three views), indicating that the temporal
averaging displaces already-precise static points away from their correct positions
rather than correcting them. The associated reduction in dynamic accuracy under Opt
is attributed to static mask boundary effects, further illuminated in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Comparison with Native 4D Architectures}
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} shows that Opt-smoothed spatial models occupy the lower-left
quadrant that native 4D architectures do not reach. At two views, Pi3+Opt achieves
0.026\,m Chamfer and 0.002\,m mean jitter, against VGGT4D's 0.051\,m Chamfer and
0.003\,m jitter, a 49\,\% reduction in Chamfer with matched or lower jitter. Static
accuracy is 76.7\,\% for Pi3+Opt against 37.1\,\% for VGGT4D. At three views,
Pi3+Opt reaches 0.033\,m Chamfer and 0.001\,m jitter against VGGT4D's 0.041\,m and
0.003\,m. At four views, Pi3+Opt achieves 0.025\,m against VGGT4D's 0.069\,m, a
64\,\% reduction, with 0.001\,m jitter
(Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}). VGGT+Opt similarly outperforms VGGT4D
at two views on every metric: 0.039\,m vs.\ 0.051\,m Chamfer, 63.5\,\% vs.\ 37.1\,\%
static accuracy, and 0.003\,m jitter matched exactly. The decoupled paradigm of strong
per-frame spatial estimation followed by targeted temporal smoothing Pareto-dominates
the native 4D architecture on the geometry--jitter trade-off at every view count for
every spatial backbone evaluated.

\subsection{Comparison with GGPT}
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} shows GGPT points to the right of every other method group.
At two views, GGPT increases MASt3R-SfM's Chamfer from 0.043\,m to 0.230\,m while
reducing mean jitter from 0.011\,m to only 0.008\,m. For VGGT, GGPT increases Chamfer
from 0.034\,m to 0.201\,m with jitter reduced from 0.009\,m to only 0.007\,m. The
GGPT\,(VGGT) reference line in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline_progression} (dotted purple,
right panel) confirms that GGPT pushes Chamfer substantially above any stage of the
spatial pipeline. Opt, by contrast, reduces MASt3R-SfM jitter from 0.011\,m to
0.001\,m while increasing Chamfer by only 0.003\,m, and reduces VGGT jitter from
0.009\,m to 0.003\,m while increasing Chamfer by only 0.005\,m
(Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}). No configuration of GGPT
Pareto-dominates the corresponding Opt configuration on the geometry--jitter
trade-off.

Figure~\ref{fig:vggt4d_smearing} reveals that VGGT4D's non-monotonic behaviour across
view counts has a clear geometric signature. At 2 views, the arm region is diffuse and
incomplete. At 3 views, the reconstruction recovers partially, but the arm appears

right of panel~(b) reflects two overlapping arm reconstructions at conflicting depths
rather than a single coherent surface. At 4 views, the arm collapses into a sweeping
curved smear covering less surface than the 3-view output, directly producing the drop
in completeness from 52.9\,\% to 30.5\,\% reported in
Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}. The smearing pattern is consistent with
the joint transformer averaging conflicting depth hypotheses from the additional camera
rather than resolving them. Spatial models do not exhibit this failure mode because
they reconstruct each frame independently.

Figure~\ref{fig:monofusion_duplicate} shows a qualitatively distinct failure mode.
The ground truth consists of two upright interacting subjects. MonoFusion produces
two spatially separated, horizontally collapsed copies of the subject pair instead.
The duplication arises from MonoFusion's iterative monocular-to-multi-view depth
alignment converging to a local optimum: the available cameras provide conflicting
depth hypotheses that the fusion procedure fails to reconcile, retaining both camera
