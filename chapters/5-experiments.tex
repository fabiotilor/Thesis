\chapter{Results}
\label{c:results}

This chapter presents the experimental results across all model families, alignment
strategies, and view counts evaluated on DexYCB and Hi4D. Section~\ref{sec:3d_baseline}
establishes the per-frame geometric performance ceiling before any temporal alignment.
Section~\ref{sec:alignment_comparison} compares the three temporal alignment strategies.
Section~\ref{sec:sparse_view} analyses sensitivity to camera count.
Section~\ref{sec:native4d_baselines} evaluates native 4D architectures and geometry
refinement. Section~\ref{sec:opt_results} presents the Opt smoothing results.
Section~\ref{sec:qualitative} provides qualitative point cloud analysis.

On DexYCB, PI3 achieves the lowest Chamfer distance (0.022\,m) and the highest static
accuracy (94.4\,\%), establishing it as the strongest spatial estimator at the 1\,cm
robotics threshold. PI3X leads on dynamic accuracy (81.4\,\%), though its baseline Chamfer of 0.028\,m is slightly higher than PI3, likely owing to its more discriminative confidence distribution retaining a sparser point set under the top-50\,\% filter, which inflates the completeness term of the Chamfer sum. MASt3R-SfM and VGGT tie on Chamfer at
0.025\,m; VGGT achieves higher static accuracy (80.7\,\% vs.\ 73.5\,\%). The four
spatial models consistently outperform both native 4D architectures: VGGT4D reaches
0.053\,m Chamfer with 57.9\,\% static accuracy, and MonoFusion 0.050\,m with 40.4\,\%
static accuracy. GGPT worsens Chamfer for every backbone without exception, a result
discussed in detail in Section~\ref{sec:native4d_baselines}.

On Hi4D, the model ordering is consistent with DexYCB. PI3 achieves the lowest Chamfer
(0.072\,m) and highest dynamic accuracy (14.4\,\%) and completeness (16.7\,\%), with
PI3X following closely (0.078\,m, 13.5\,\%). Dynamic accuracy values on Hi4D are
substantially lower than on DexYCB across all models, reflecting the greater difficulty
of close human--human interaction sequences with stronger self-occlusions and larger
non-rigid motion. VGGT4D (0.230\,m) and MonoFusion (0.356\,m) degrade more severely
than spatial models on this out-of-distribution dataset. Static accuracy and completeness
are not reported for Hi4D, since the background is masked during preprocessing and all
evaluated points belong to dynamic subject regions (Section~\ref{sec:datasets}).



\section{Alignment Strategy comparison}
\label{sec:alignment_comparison}

Lifting per-frame reconstructions into a temporally coherent 4D representation requires
bringing independently reconstructed frames into a shared coordinate system. The three
strategies introduced in Section~\ref{sec:alignment_strategies} differ in how they
establish this shared system, and consequently in how much geometric distortion they
introduce. Table~\ref{tab:strategy_extended} compares all three on DexYCB and Hi4D,
reporting Chamfer distance, $\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$, and camera pose metrics
pooled over view counts. $\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$ isolates the geometric cost of
the alignment itself: it measures the difference between the sequence-level Chamfer
distance after temporal alignment and the per-frame Chamfer distance under independent
ground-truth registration (Section~\ref{sec:metrics}). Camera pose metrics serve as an
additional indicator of alignment quality, since the same Umeyama transformations
estimated for the point clouds are applied to the cameras.

On DexYCB, the three strategies produce closely matched Chamfer distances across all
models, with differences of at most 0.001\,m. Strategy 3 achieves the lowest
$\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$ in most cases and the best camera pose metrics, indicating
that alignment graph optimisation over all $\mathcal{O}(T^2)$ pairwise edges introduces less
geometric distortion than single-path strategies. For MASt3R-SfM, Strategy 3 reduces
$\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$ from 0.007 (Strategy 1) to 0.005 and achieves the best
ATE (0.124) and RPE (0.067). For VGGT, Strategy 2 produces the lowest Chamfer (0.030\,m)
and $\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$ (0.005). No single strategy dominates across all
models and metrics; the differences are modest throughout. Strategy 2 is selected as
the alignment basis for the temporal smoothing refinement in
Section~\ref{sec:opt_results}, with the rationale for this choice discussed in
Chapter~\ref{c:discussion}.

On Hi4D, the alignment strategies are applied directly to the dynamic scene points,
since no static background is available after preprocessing (Section~\ref{sec:datasets}).
The strategies were designed to exploit static correspondences as stable anchors for
temporal registration; applying them to dynamic content instead is an approximation that
relies on inter-frame motion being sufficiently small for the alignments to remain
meaningful. For MASt3R-SfM, the results show a clear degradation with more complex
strategies: Chamfer increases from 0.279\,m under Strategy 1 to 0.302\,m under
Strategy 2 and 0.330\,m under Strategy 3, with $\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$ rising
correspondingly from 0.121 to 0.172. For VGGT, Strategy 1 and Strategy 2 produce
identical Chamfer (0.099\,m), with Strategy 3 increasing to 0.107\,m. For PI3 and PI3X,
all three strategies produce identical results across every metric. The static
correspondence signal that drives differentiation between the strategies is absent on
Hi4D, and the dynamic points provide insufficient variation for the alignment to produce
different outcomes across strategy variants. Hi4D results are therefore reported for
completeness but reflect the performance of the underlying models rather than the
properties of the alignment strategies.

Spatial models show consistent improvement with additional views on DexYCB. PI3's

static accuracy rising from 71.2\,\% to 87.0\,\%. PI3X exhibits the strongest gains in

88.5\,\% at four views, while static accuracy reaches 94.3\,\%---the highest value
across all models and view counts. MASt3R-SfM benefits most from the addition of a
third camera, where Chamfer drops from 0.043\,m to 0.023\,m and static accuracy
increases from 46.3\,\% to 73.8\,\%; four-view Chamfer remains competitive at
0.026\,m. VGGT improves monotonically, with Chamfer decreasing from 0.034\,m at two
views to 0.027\,m at four views and static accuracy rising from 64.2\,\% to 74.1\,\%.

0.051\,m at two views to 0.041\,m at three views as the additional camera provides
useful multi-view overlap, but rises sharply to 0.069\,m at four views. Static

individually well-placed but that a large portion of the surface is lost.

dynamic arm region in VGGT4D outputs, which is consistent with the non-monotonic
completeness pattern observed across view counts.

two views to 0.031\,m at both three and four views. However, MonoFusion's low Chamfer
at three and four views does not translate to proportionally high precision at the
1\,cm threshold: static accuracy reaches 54.4\,\% at three views and 62.0\,\% at four
views, compared to 89.7\,\% and 87.0\,\% for PI3 at the same view counts. At two views,
static accuracy drops to 15.5\,\% with static completeness at 19.9\,\%,
consistent with the duplicated-subject artefacts documented in
Section~\ref{sec:qualitative}.

\section{Native 4D Architectures and Geometry Refinement}
\label{sec:native4d_baselines}

\paragraph{VGGT4D.}
VGGT4D enforces sequence-level temporal consistency through a joint transformer
architecture, but does so at a substantial geometric cost. Unlike the spatial models,
which reconstruct each frame independently and require a temporal alignment strategy
(Chapter~\ref{c:methodology}) to produce a coherent 4D sequence, VGGT4D processes the
full multi-view video jointly and outputs a natively consistent 4D reconstruction.
To assess its raw geometric fidelity, its native 4D output is aligned independently
to the ground truth frame by frame---the same per-frame registration applied to spatial
model baselines---providing a best-case measure of how accurately VGGT4D recovers
scene geometry regardless of its temporal consistency properties.

Table~\ref{tab:baseline_control_ggpt} shows that under this per-frame evaluation,
VGGT4D achieves a Chamfer distance of 0.053\,m on DexYCB---141\,\% higher than PI3
(0.022\,m) and 112\,\% higher than MASt3R-SfM and VGGT (both 0.025\,m). On Hi4D,
VGGT4D reaches 0.230\,m compared to PI3's 0.072\,m, a 219\,\% increase. The geometric
gap therefore exists at the per-frame level, before any temporal alignment enters the
picture for either model family.

When spatial models are lifted to 4D via the temporal alignment strategies of
Chapter~\ref{c:methodology} and compared against VGGT4D's native 4D output,
the gap persists across all view counts. Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}
shows that at two views, VGGT4D achieves 0.051\,m Chamfer, compared to 0.031\,m for
PI3 Strategy 2 and 0.034\,m for VGGT Strategy 2. At three views, VGGT4D reaches
0.041\,m, against PI3's 0.035\,m and VGGT's 0.028\,m. At four views, VGGT4D's Chamfer
rises to 0.069\,m---higher than its own two-view result---while PI3 Strategy 2 achieves
0.026\,m and VGGT Strategy 2 achieves 0.027\,m. Static accuracy follows the same
pattern: at four views, VGGT4D reaches 69.1\,\%, compared to 87.0\,\% for PI3
Strategy 2 and 74.1\,\% for VGGT Strategy 2. Jitter is reduced relative to unsmoothed
spatial models (0.003\,m mean at two and three views,
Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}), but Section~\ref{sec:opt_results} shows
that spatial models with Opt smoothing reach comparable or lower jitter without the
geometric cost.

\paragraph{MonoFusion.}
MonoFusion achieves the lowest jitter across all evaluated methods at two and four views
(0.001\,m mean jitter, Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}). Geometric fidelity
is substantially weaker, however. Table~\ref{tab:baseline_control_ggpt} reports
MonoFusion's per-frame Chamfer at 0.050\,m on DexYCB, with static accuracy of 40.4\,\%
and completeness of 46.0\,\%---the lowest among all evaluated models. On Hi4D,
performance degrades further to 0.356\,m Chamfer. At two views,
Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb} shows static accuracy dropping to 15.5\,\%
with static completeness at 19.9\,\%, consistent with the duplicated-subject artefacts
documented in Section~\ref{sec:qualitative} and illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:monofusion_duplicate}. These artefacts arise from the
local-optima susceptibility of MonoFusion's iterative depth alignment under insufficient
multi-view overlap, described in Section~\ref{sec:temporal_models}. GGPT applied to
MonoFusion on Hi4D is absent from Table~\ref{tab:baseline_control_ggpt} because the
underlying artefacts render the native output unreliable as a refinement input.

\paragraph{GGPT.}
GGPT consistently worsens Chamfer distance across every backbone and both datasets.
Table~\ref{tab:baseline_control_ggpt} shows that on DexYCB, GGPT increases
MASt3R-SfM's Chamfer from 0.025\,m to 0.167\,m ($\times$6.7), VGGT's from 0.025\,m
to 0.203\,m ($\times$8.1), PI3's from 0.022\,m to 0.105\,m ($\times$4.8), and PI3X's
from 0.028\,m to 0.107\,m ($\times$3.8). On Hi4D, the degradation is more severe:
VGGT's Chamfer rises from 0.095\,m to 1.510\,m ($\times$15.9) and VGGT4D's from
0.230\,m to 1.057\,m ($\times$4.6). The negative $\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$ values
for GGPT on Hi4D visible in Table~\ref{tab:strategy_extended}---for example,
$-0.048$ for MASt3R-SfM GGPT(S3) and $-0.078$ for VGGT GGPT(S3)---do not indicate
genuine geometric improvement. Rather, GGPT's geometry-destructive effect is so
dominant that the per-frame GGPT baseline is already substantially worse than the
aligned 4D output, making any temporal aggregation appear to reduce Chamfer relative to
that degraded starting point. In most configurations, GGPT also reduces accuracy and
completeness substantially, particularly on dynamic regions. GGPT is therefore
unsuitable for geometry-critical applications in the sparse-view dynamic setting
evaluated here.


\section{Temporal smoothing refinement}
\label{sec:opt_results}
As established in Section~\ref{sec:temporal_models}, per-frame reconstruction
produces frame-to-frame variation in static scene regions that is purely an
artefact of reconstruction noise rather than genuine scene motion. The temporal
alignment strategies of Chapter~\ref{c:methodology} lift these per-frame
reconstructions into a common coordinate system, producing a 4D representation,
but do not address the underlying per-pixel depth instability that causes jitter,
since each frame remains independently reconstructed. This residual instability
is the target of the confidence-weighted temporal smoothing method (Opt), whose
effect on reconstruction quality and temporal consistency is evaluated in the
following results. Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} situates all evaluated methods in the
geometry--jitter trade-off space. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline_progression} traces
the effect of each pipeline stage on both jitter and Chamfer at two views, with
native 4D architectures and GGPT shown as reference baselines. Numerical detail
is drawn from Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}.

counts. Each point represents one model--method combination plotted on logarithmic
axes. Opt-smoothed spatial models (blue stars) cluster in the lower-left quadrant,
achieving both low Chamfer distance and low mean jitter simultaneously. GGPT points
lie to the right of all spatial model configurations, reflecting substantial Chamfer

relative to unsmoothed spatial models but at substantially higher Chamfer. The

\FloatBarrier

\subsection{High-frequency Jitter Elimination}
HF jitter is defined in Section~\ref{sec:metrics} as second-order
temporal acceleration, sensitive to rapid oscillations rather than
slow drift.
The reduction in HF jitter is the strongest result in
Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}.
Across every model and every view count, Opt reduces HF jitter to a
value that rounds to 0.000\,m in Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb},
compared to baseline Strategy-2 values ranging from 0.008\,m to
0.019\,m depending on model and view count.
The native 4D architecture VGGT4D achieves only 0.005\,m at two views.
This result is theoretically expected: as the Gaussian temporal filter
defined in Section~\ref{sec:opt_method} acts as a low-pass filter over
the temporal frequency domain, it attenuates high-frequency oscillations
by construction, and the second-order finite difference used to measure
HF jitter is precisely the signal it suppresses.

\subsection{Mean Jitter Reduction}
Mean jitter captures lower-frequency instability that the Gaussian
attenuates less completely than high-frequency oscillations.
Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline_progression} shows a consistent reduction
across all four spatial models.
At two views, MASt3R-SfM mean jitter falls from 0.011\,m
(Strategy 2) to 0.001\,m (Opt), a 91\,\% reduction consistent with
its wide temporal window ($\sigma = 6$).
VGGT falls from 0.009\,m to 0.003\,m (67\,\%), PI3 from 0.006\,m to
0.002\,m (67\,\%), and PI3X from 0.005\,m to 0.002\,m (60\,\%).
The reductions are consistent across three and four views.
MASt3R-SfM and VGGT reach 0.001\,m mean jitter at every view count,
approaching MonoFusion's 0.001\,m level.
All four Opt-smoothed models cross below the VGGT4D mean jitter
reference line of 0.003\,m visible in
Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline_progression}.


\subsection{Drift and the Limits of Temporal Smoothing}
Drift measures a qualitatively different failure mode from jitter.
Where jitter is high-frequency noise, drift is a low-frequency bias:
a slow accumulation of pose estimation errors that displaces static
anchors monotonically over the sequence. Because the Gaussian temporal
filter defined in Section~\ref{sec:opt_method} is a low-pass filter,
it is by design insensitive to this frequency range, and no reduction
in drift should be expected from Opt. Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}
confirms this: Opt does not reliably reduce drift, and in some cases
increases it. Where drift increases under Opt, the temporal averaging
spreads the low-frequency alignment bias across the smoothing window
rather than correcting it, since the underlying coordinate system errors
that cause drift are not addressed by the filter.
MonoFusion achieves consistently near-zero drift (0.001\,m at every
view count).
GGPT\,(S2) reduces drift relative to Strategy~2 in several cases,
but not without substantially degrading Chamfer distance, and the
improvement is not consistent across view counts or backbones.

\subsection{Geometric Cost}
The right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline_progression} shows that the Chamfer
distance of three of the four models remains near or below the Strategy~2 level after
Opt smoothing. For MASt3R-SfM, Chamfer improves slightly from 0.023\,m to 0.022\,m
at three views and is unchanged at 0.026\,m at four views. For VGGT, Chamfer is
unchanged at 0.028\,m at three views and increases marginally from 0.027\,m to
0.029\,m at four views. For PI3, Chamfer improves at two views from 0.031\,m to
0.026\,m and remains competitive at three and four views. PI3X is the exception: Opt
increases Chamfer from 0.027\,m to 0.040\,m at two views (+48\,\%) and from 0.032\,m
to 0.035--0.036\,m at three and four views (+9--13\,\%).

The full per-view breakdown in Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb} additionally
reveals the effect on static accuracy and completeness, showing whether the temporal
averaging bias introduced by Opt acts as a corrective or a degrading influence on
static reconstruction quality. For MASt3R-SfM and PI3, the bias is corrective: at
three and four views, static accuracy and completeness both improve under Opt
(MASt3R-SfM: 73.8\,\%\,$\to$\,75.5\,\% accuracy and 66.3\,\%\,$\to$\,68.3\,\%
completeness at three views; PI3: 71.2\,\%\,$\to$\,76.7\,\% accuracy and
62.2\,\%\,$\to$\,67.5\,\% completeness at two views), indicating that the temporal
mean pulls noisy static point estimates closer to their true positions. For VGGT, the
effect is approximately neutral, with static accuracy and completeness changing by less
than one percentage point across view counts. For PI3X, both static accuracy and
completeness degrade consistently (e.g., 90.9\,\%\,$\to$\,85.2\,\% accuracy and
75.7\,\%\,$\to$\,72.1\,\% completeness at three views), indicating that the temporal
averaging displaces already-precise static points away from their correct positions
rather than correcting them. The associated reduction in dynamic accuracy under Opt
is attributed to static mask boundary effects, further illuminated in~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Comparison with Native 4D Architectures}
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} shows that Opt-smoothed spatial models occupy the lower-left
quadrant that native 4D architectures do not reach. At two views, PI3+Opt achieves
0.026\,m Chamfer and 0.002\,m mean jitter, against VGGT4D's 0.051\,m Chamfer and
0.003\,m jitter, a 49\,\% reduction in Chamfer with matched or lower jitter. Static
accuracy is 76.7\,\% for PI3+Opt against 37.1\,\% for VGGT4D. At three views,
PI3+Opt reaches 0.033\,m Chamfer and 0.001\,m jitter against VGGT4D's 0.041\,m and
0.003\,m. At four views, PI3+Opt achieves 0.025\,m against VGGT4D's 0.069\,m, a
64\,\% reduction, with 0.001\,m jitter
(Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}). VGGT+Opt similarly outperforms VGGT4D
at two views on every metric: 0.039\,m vs.\ 0.051\,m Chamfer, 63.5\,\% vs.\ 37.1\,\%
static accuracy, and 0.003\,m jitter matched exactly. The decoupled paradigm of strong
per-frame spatial estimation followed by targeted temporal smoothing Pareto-dominates
the native 4D architecture on the geometry--jitter trade-off at every view count for
every spatial backbone evaluated.

\subsection{Comparison with GGPT}
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} shows GGPT points to the right of every other method group.
At two views, GGPT increases MASt3R-SfM's Chamfer from 0.043\,m to 0.230\,m while
reducing mean jitter from 0.011\,m to only 0.008\,m. For VGGT, GGPT increases Chamfer
from 0.034\,m to 0.201\,m with jitter reduced from 0.009\,m to only 0.007\,m. The
GGPT\,(VGGT) reference line in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline_progression} (dotted purple,
right panel) confirms that GGPT pushes Chamfer substantially above any stage of the
spatial pipeline. Opt, by contrast, reduces MASt3R-SfM jitter from 0.011\,m to
0.001\,m while increasing Chamfer by only 0.003\,m, and reduces VGGT jitter from
0.009\,m to 0.003\,m while increasing Chamfer by only 0.005\,m
(Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}). No configuration of GGPT
Pareto-dominates the corresponding Opt configuration on the geometry--jitter
trade-off.

Figure~\ref{fig:vggt4d_smearing} reveals that VGGT4D's non-monotonic behaviour across
view counts has a clear geometric signature. At 2 views, the arm region is diffuse and
incomplete. At 3 views, the reconstruction recovers partially, but the arm appears
duplicated at a second depth layer---the increased point density visible toward the
right of panel~(b) reflects two overlapping arm reconstructions at conflicting depths
rather than a single coherent surface. At 4 views, the arm collapses into a sweeping
curved smear covering less surface than the 3-view output, directly producing the drop
in completeness from 52.9\,\% to 30.5\,\% reported in
Table~\ref{tab:global_performance_dex_ycb}. The smearing pattern is consistent with
the joint transformer averaging conflicting depth hypotheses from the additional camera
rather than resolving them. Spatial models do not exhibit this failure mode because
they reconstruct each frame independently.

Figure~\ref{fig:monofusion_duplicate} shows a qualitatively distinct failure mode.
The ground truth consists of two upright interacting subjects. MonoFusion produces
two spatially separated, horizontally collapsed copies of the subject pair instead.
The duplication arises from MonoFusion's iterative monocular-to-multi-view depth
alignment converging to a local optimum: the available cameras provide conflicting
depth hypotheses that the fusion procedure fails to reconcile, retaining both camera
estimates as spatially offset outputs rather than a single consistent reconstruction.
The failure persists across all view counts evaluated on Hi4D, consistent with the
wide camera spacing and large subject-to-camera distance of the Hi4D rig providing
insufficient surface overlap for the alignment to converge, regardless of the number
of available cameras evauluated in this thesis.
