\chapter{Introduction}\label{c:introduction}

Recovering the three-dimensional structure of a scene from visual observations is a foundational problem in computer vision with critical applications in robotics and autonomous navigation~\cite{hartley2004multiple}. While spatial-first foundation models such as MASt3R~\cite{mast3r} and PI3X~\cite{pi3} have achieved remarkable per-frame geometric fidelity, their application to 4D sequences - where temporal coherence is as vital as spatial accuracy - remains an open challenge. In sparse-view configurations (2-4 cameras), the problem is bifurcated: spatial models lack the temporal grounding to prevent high-frequency jitter, whereas dedicated 4D models, such as VGGT4D~\cite{vggt4d}, introduce temporal priors that risk over-smoothing geometry or creating "motion-blur" artifacts.

This thesis investigates the transition from per-frame 3D reconstruction to sequence-level 4D modeling. We evaluate whether the current generation of foundation models can maintain metric integrity across time or if the introduction of temporal constraints inevitably degrades geometric precision.

\section{Background and Motivation}

Classical multi-view reconstruction methods such as SfM~\cite{schoenberger2016sfm} provide high rigour but struggle with the textureless regions and dynamic occlusions inherent in human-centric environments. Learning-based spatial models like DUSt3R~\cite{dust3r} and PI3~\cite{pi3} have largely superseded these by regressing dense pointmaps directly, yet they treat video as a collection of independent snapshots. This independence results in significant temporal instability when these models are deployed on robotic platforms requiring smooth trajectory estimation.

To address this, temporal-first architectures like VGGT4D~\cite{vggt4d} and MonoFusion~\cite{monofusion} incorporate motion cues to encourage sequence-level consistency. However, the nature of this consistency - whether it acts as a beneficial geometric anchor or a destructive low-pass filter - is poorly understood in sparse-view settings. There is a critical need for a benchmarking framework that quantifies the "temporal cost" of these various architectural choices.

\section{Problem Statement and Contributions}

The central objective of this work is to characterize the performance of 3D and 4D foundation models under the strict hardware constraints of sparse-view (2–4 camera) setups. We focus on measuring the divergence between per-frame accuracy and sequence-level stability, identifying where hierarchical vs. graph-based alignment strategies succeed in mitigating drift.

The main contributions of this work are:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{4D Benchmarking Framework.} A unified evaluation pipeline for sparse multi-view video that subjects six base models (MAST3R-SfM, VGGT, PI3, PI3X, VGGT4D, and MonoFusion) to identical data-loading and semantic masking conditions across the DEX-YCB, Hi4D, and 4D-DRESS datasets. Crucially, this framework evaluates the impact of applying the GGPT \cite{ggpt} refinement strategy across these architectures (excluding MonoFusion), providing a direct comparison between raw spatial outputs and geometry-grounded 4D sequences.

\item \textbf{The Delta-Consistency Metric.} We formalize a novel quantitative measure to isolate the "temporal cost" of sequence alignment. This metric, $\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$ (see \Cref{eq:delta_consistency}), captures the divergence in geometric fidelity when per-frame reconstructions are transformed from independent, local coordinate systems into a globally consistent 3D reference frame. This value exposes the spatial degradation caused by alignment drift and temporal constraints.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:delta_consistency}
    \Delta_{\text{consistency}} = \text{Chamfer}_{\text{4D}} - \text{Chamfer}_{\text{3D}}
\end{equation}

\item \textbf{Comparative Alignment Study.} A rigorous analysis of three temporal alignment strategies, highlighting a performance split: identifying hierarchical binary-tree methods as superior for static scene completeness and graph-based optimization (PGO) as superior for dynamic accuracy and minimizing $\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$.
