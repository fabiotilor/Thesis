\chapter{Introduction}\label{c:introduction}

Recovering the three-dimensional structure of a dynamic scene from visual observations is a
foundational challenge in computer vision, with direct implications for robotics and
autonomous navigation~\cite{hartley2004multiple}. When observations span a temporal
sequence, the problem extends to 4D reconstruction. This requires recovering geometry that is not only
spatially accurate per frame, but also consistent across time. Sparse-view settings, which in this thesis features only two to
four cameras observing the scene, are particularly demanding. In these scenarios, limited overlap, short baselines,
and textureless surfaces cause classical Structure-from-Motion pipelines to fail outright or
produce drift-prone geometry unsuitable for downstream tasks such as grasping, object
tracking, and robotic manipulation~\cite{schoenberger2016sfm, hartley2004multiple}.

Recent spatial foundation models address many of these failure modes. MASt3R-SfM~\cite{mast3r},
VGGT~\cite{vggt}, PI3, and PI3X~\cite{pi3} regress dense 3D pointmaps directly from images,
using learned priors to fill in low-texture and low-overlap regions where classical methods
collapse. PI3 and PI3X in particular achieve the highest accuracy and completeness at a
strict 1\,cm robotics threshold among all models evaluated in this thesis. As purely spatial
estimators, however, these models process each frame independently. Assembled
frame-by-frame, their outputs exhibit high-frequency jitter and scale fluctuations that
render them unsuitable for applications requiring smooth, temporally consistent reconstruction.

Two directions have been explored to close this gap. Geometry-refinement approaches such as
GGPT~\cite{ggpt} ground spatial predictions with incomplete SfM-derived guidance, but do not
generalise to sparse-view dynamic scenes, worsening Chamfer distance by $3.8$--$15.9\times$
across all evaluated backbones and datasets. Dedicated 4D architectures such as VGGT4D~\cite{vggt4d} and
MonoFusion~\cite{monofusion} enforce sequence-level consistency directly, but sacrifice
geometric fidelity to do so: even under a best-case per-frame alignment to ground truth,
VGGT4D's Chamfer distance remains 62--192\,\% higher than the VGGT baseline across
two-to-four view configurations, and MonoFusion produces duplicated subject artefacts rather
than coherent reconstructions under the same conditions.

No existing approach simultaneously achieves geometric fidelity and temporal consistency
under sparse-view dynamic conditions. The core challenge is that existing methods enforce temporal consistency globally within the core reconstruction loop. Whether through early attention-masking or global affine alignment, treating the sequence as a single optimization problem forces a trade-off: it penalizes real dynamic motion, ultimately sacrificing the frame-level geometric fidelity that spatial foundation models naturally provide.

Motivated by this observation, this thesis proposes a decoupled paradigm: take a strong
per-frame spatial estimator, align its outputs into a common coordinate frame through a
lightweight temporal alignment strategy, and apply a confidence-weighted Gaussian filter
exclusively to static regions to suppress jitter without touching dynamic pixels. Evaluated
on DexYCB~\cite{dexycb} and Hi4D~\cite{hi4d}, this approach achieves jitter comparable to
native 4D architectures while retaining the geometric fidelity of the underlying per-frame
estimator---a trade-off that neither geometry-refinement nor native 4D baselines match.

\section{Contributions}\label{s:contributions}

The main contributions of this thesis are:

\begin{itemize}

  \item \textbf{Unified 4D Benchmarking Framework.}
        A structured evaluation pipeline subjects six reconstruction models to identical
        data-loading, semantic masking, and metric-computation conditions across DexYCB~\cite{dexycb}
        and Hi4D~\cite{hi4d}, measuring per-frame and sequence-level performance via Chamfer
        distance, accuracy, completeness, temporal jitter, drift, and camera pose error
        (see \Cref{c:experimental_setup}).

\item \textbf{Comparative Temporal Alignment Study.}
        Three graph-based strategies for lifting independent per-frame 3D reconstructions 
        into a unified, sequence-level 4D coordinate system are evaluated. The study 
        characterises how these alignment strategies impact the underlying model's 
        geometric reconstruction quality by isolating the structural distortion 
        introduced purely by temporal registration ($\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$) (see \Cref{sec:alignment_strategies}).

\item \textbf{Confidence-Weighted Temporal Smoothing.}
    A training-free post-processing method reduces frame-to-frame instability for any
    model that produces dense per-frame pointmaps by applying confidence-weighted
    Gaussian smoothing exclusively to static regions, leaving dynamic pixels untouched.
    The method achieves jitter comparable to native 4D architectures while retaining
    the geometric fidelity of the underlying per-frame estimator---a trade-off that
    native 4D architectures consistently fail to match (see \Cref{sec:opt}).

\end{itemize}
