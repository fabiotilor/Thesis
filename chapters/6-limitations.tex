\chapter{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{c:discussion}

This thesis set out to answer a single question: can sparse-view dynamic reconstruction
achieve both geometric fidelity and temporal consistency simultaneously, without
sacrificing one for the other? The results of Chapter~\ref{c:results} provide a clear
answer. The decoupled paradigm of a strong per-frame spatial estimator, lightweight
temporal alignment, and targeted static-region smoothing consistently achieves both
properties across every backbone and every view count evaluated. No existing alternative
matches this trade-off. This chapter interprets why, identifies what remains unsolved,
and proposes directions for future work.

\section{Architecture family analysis}
\label{sec:architecture_analysis}

The central finding of Chapter~\ref{c:results} is that native 4D architectures fail to
close the geometry--jitter trade-off that spatial models with Opt smoothing achieve.
Understanding why requires examining the architectural assumptions each family makes.

\subsection{Why Spatial Models Succeed}
Spatial foundation models reconstruct each frame independently, using learned priors to
recover dense geometry from sparse observations. Independence is a strength: each frame
is solved without any assumption about what neighbouring frames look like, so
reconstruction errors do not propagate across time. PI3 achieves 0.022\,m Chamfer and
94.4\,\% static accuracy on DexYCB at the per-frame level, the highest geometric
fidelity among all evaluated models, precisely because no temporal constraint forces it
to compromise geometric detail for sequence consistency.

\subsection{Why Native 4D Architectures Fail}
VGGT4D processes the full multi-view video jointly, enforcing temporal consistency
through a shared transformer representation. The cost is geometric: VGGT4D's per-frame
Chamfer is 141\,\% higher than PI3 on DexYCB even under best-case per-frame alignment
to ground truth. Although VGGT4D explicitly attempts to decouple static and dynamic
regions through attention-based dynamic cue mining~\cite{vggt4d}, the qualitative
analysis of Section~\ref{sec:qualitative} shows that the decoupling is incomplete under
sparse-view conditions. At 3 views, the arm region appears duplicated at a second depth
layer. At 4 views, the arm collapses into a sweeping smear. Both artefacts are
consistent with temporal averaging of conflicting depth hypotheses: when the joint
architecture receives a new viewpoint whose depth estimates conflict with existing ones,
it averages rather than resolves, displacing the reconstructed surface and reducing
completeness. The non-monotonic completeness pattern of 52.9\,\% at 3 views falling to
30.5\,\% at 4 views is a direct consequence. Native 4D architectures are therefore not
a principled solution to the geometry--jitter trade-off under sparse-view conditions;
they trade one problem for another.

MonoFusion exhibits a different failure mode. Its iterative monocular-to-multi-view
depth alignment is susceptible to local optima under the wide camera spacing and large
subject-to-camera distance of both evaluated datasets, producing duplicated-subject
artefacts rather than coherent reconstructions. MonoFusion achieves the lowest drift
(0.001\,m at every view count) and competitive mean jitter, but its geometric fidelity
is the weakest among all evaluated models: 0.050\,m Chamfer on DexYCB and 0.356\,m on
Hi4D.

\subsection{Why GGPT is Unsuitable for Geometry-Critical Applications}
GGPT combines dense pointmap predictions with incomplete SfM-derived geometric cues
through a transformer-based refinement network. The approach degrades geometry
catastrophically across every backbone and both datasets: Chamfer worsens by factors of
3.8 to 15.9. The negative $\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$ values observed for GGPT on
Hi4D are not evidence of genuine improvement; they arise because GGPT's per-frame
geometry is already so degraded that temporal aggregation appears to reduce Chamfer
relative to that degraded baseline. GGPT provides marginal jitter reduction: at two
views, MASt3R-SfM jitter falls from 0.011\,m to 0.008\,m under GGPT, but at a
geometric cost that no robotics application requiring 1\,cm precision could accept.
The incomplete SfM cues that GGPT relies on are derived from the same sparse-view input
that already challenges spatial models; grounding a refinement network on unreliable
geometric evidence does not improve accuracy and evidently introduces systematic
distortion.

\section{The decoupled framework paradigm}
\label{sec:decoupled_paradigm}

The results of Chapter~\ref{c:results} provide consistent evidence for a design
principle: separating the problem of geometric reconstruction from the problem of
temporal consistency, and solving each with the right tool, outperforms joint
end-to-end approaches.

The argument has three parts. First, spatial foundation models achieve higher geometric
fidelity than native 4D architectures at the per-frame level, before any temporal
processing is applied. The gap is large: PI3 outperforms VGGT4D by 141\,\% on DexYCB
Chamfer at the per-frame level. Second, temporal consistency can be added after the
fact without destroying geometry, provided the smoothing is applied only where it is
valid, namely static regions. Opt reduces mean jitter by 60--91\,\% across four
backbones while leaving Chamfer flat or improved for three of the four. Third, the
resulting pipeline Pareto-dominates native 4D architectures on the geometry--jitter
trade-off at every view count for every backbone evaluated. PI3+Opt achieves 0.025\,m
Chamfer and 0.001\,m mean jitter at four views; VGGT4D achieves 0.069\,m Chamfer and
0.003\,m jitter at the same view count.

The key insight is that geometric accuracy and temporal consistency conflict only when
temporal constraints are applied globally, penalising dynamic regions where motion is
real and should be preserved. The decoupled paradigm avoids this conflict by design:
the spatial estimator is free to maximise geometric fidelity without any temporal
pressure, and Opt is free to maximise temporal consistency without any geometric
pressure, because it operates exclusively on static regions where consistency is
physically guaranteed.

Strategy 2 is selected as the alignment basis for Opt over Strategy 3 despite Strategy
3 achieving marginally better $\Delta_{\text{consistency}}$ in most cases. The
differences between strategies are modest throughout, at most 0.002\,m Chamfer on
DexYCB, and Strategy 2 provides a better practical balance between computational cost
and geometric stability. Strategy 3 requires $\mathcal{O}(T^2)$ pairwise alignments
and iterative geodesic averaging, making it substantially slower without proportionate
benefit. Strategy 2's $\mathcal{O}(T \log T)$ complexity and consistent performance
across all four backbones make it the more suitable foundation for a post-processing
pipeline intended for practical deployment.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Four limitations of the proposed approach warrant explicit discussion.

\subsection{Per-Backbone Parameter Calibration}
Opt requires per-backbone values of $\sigma$ and $\alpha$, selected by sweeping over a
grid and measuring the geometry--jitter trade-off on DexYCB. The selected
configurations ($\sigma = 6$, $\alpha = 1.0$ for MASt3R-SfM; $\sigma = 4$,
$\alpha = 1.0$ for PI3, PI3X, and VGGT) represent single operating points on the
Pareto frontier of each backbone. Individual subjects within DexYCB already exhibit at
least two distinct jitter profiles that would benefit from different parameters; a
globally fixed configuration is a compromise. More fundamentally, the jitter level
produced by a spatial backbone depends on the reconstruction confidence of that
backbone in the current scene, which varies with scene type, texture, lighting, and
the distribution of the backbone's training data. A parameter set calibrated on DexYCB
manipulation sequences may not transfer to surgical, outdoor, or industrial scenes
without recalibration.

\subsection{Drift is Not Corrected}
Opt suppresses high-frequency jitter and reduces mean jitter substantially, but does
not reliably reduce drift. Drift is a slow, monotonic accumulation of pose estimation
errors that displaces static anchors over the sequence. Because the Gaussian filter
operates symmetrically in time, it attenuates rapid oscillations effectively but cannot
correct a bias that accumulates monotonically. At two views, PI3 drift increases from
0.007\,m under Strategy 2 to 0.034\,m under Opt, and VGGT drift falls only marginally
from 0.060\,m to 0.054\,m. Correcting drift requires a mechanism that operates
globally across the sequence rather than locally in time, such as the
anchor-constrained optimisation described in Section~\ref{sec:future_work}.

\subsection{Static Mask Quality}
The SAM2-derived static masks are a critical dependency of both the alignment
strategies and Opt. Mask quality degrades in several scenarios: fast motion that
exceeds the frame-difference signal, textureless dynamic surfaces that produce weak
optical flow, and scene boundaries where static and dynamic regions are spatially
adjacent. Boundary pixels at the static-dynamic boundary are incidentally affected by
Opt smoothing: projecting smoothed boundary points back into 2D shifts their apparent
class assignment, producing a reduction in reported dynamic accuracy beyond the direct
effect of the filter. This boundary reclassification effect is most visible for PI3X,
where dynamic accuracy falls substantially under Opt despite the filter operating only
on classified static pixels.

\subsection{Evaluation Scope}
Opt is evaluated exclusively on DexYCB, since Hi4D provides no static background
against which jitter metrics can be computed. The temporal consistency results
therefore rest on a single dataset covering human--object manipulation under controlled
laboratory conditions. Whether the 60--91\,\% jitter reductions reported here
generalise to outdoor scenes, surgical environments, or datasets with different camera
geometries and motion profiles remains an open question. The alignment strategy
comparison covers both datasets, but Hi4D results reflect the performance of the
underlying models rather than the properties of the strategies, since static
correspondences are absent after preprocessing.

\section{Future work}
\label{sec:future_work}

This thesis established a foundation: spatial foundation models can be lifted to
temporally consistent 4D reconstruction through lightweight alignment and a
training-free temporal low-pass filter, without sacrificing geometric fidelity. The
remaining open problems are clear. Drift is not corrected. The 2-view to 4-view
performance gap is not closed. Evaluation is limited to fixed, calibrated camera rigs
on two datasets. Future work can extend this foundation in four directions: improving
the smoothing method itself, correcting drift, broadening evaluation to moving cameras
and more diverse scenes, and exploring whether the decoupled paradigm can be pushed
further through learned refinement.

\subsection{Inference-Time Adaptive Parameter Selection}
The per-backbone parameter calibration limitation motivates replacing the offline sweep
with an adaptive procedure that selects $\sigma$ and $\alpha$ at inference time from
the current sequence. The required signal is available without ground truth: the
per-frame jitter of static anchors, computable from the aligned pointmaps before Opt
is applied, directly reflects the instability that $\sigma$ must suppress. A wider
$\sigma$ is appropriate when anchor jitter is high; a narrower $\sigma$ when it is
stable. Fitting $\sigma$ to the observed jitter profile of each sequence would remove
the dataset-specific calibration requirement and produce a method that generalises
without recalibration.

\subsection{Global Anchor-Constrained Drift Correction}
The drift limitation motivates a second post-processing stage applied after Opt.
After Opt suppresses jitter, static scene anchors are nearly stable across frames,
making their world-space positions reliably identifiable. The current alignment
strategies minimise pairwise alignment error sequentially, which allows drift to
accumulate along the propagation path. Treating static anchor positions as fixed
landmarks and solving simultaneously for all per-frame similarity transforms would
break this sequential structure: every frame is coupled to every anchor at once,
leaving no path along which errors can propagate monotonically. This direction is
structurally analogous to classical bundle adjustment applied to already-reconstructed
pointmaps rather than raw image correspondences, and requires no additional training
data.

\subsection{Reprojection-Error Cross-View Refinement}
After drift correction, the stabilised static anchors and known camera intrinsics
enable a further refinement stage: minimising cross-view reprojection error to improve
geometric consistency across cameras. In the 2-view regime, the results of
Section~\ref{sec:sparse_view} show a substantial performance gap relative to 4 views.
The spatial foundation models are not trained to produce outputs that agree across
views; a post-processing stage that penalises reprojection inconsistency could close
this gap without requiring additional cameras or retraining. Reprojection-based
refinement is training-free and model-agnostic, and directly addresses the cross-view
surface alignment problem identified in the original project description as the central
unsolved challenge of sparse-view reconstruction.

\subsection{Extension to Moving Cameras and Broader Evaluation}
This thesis evaluates exclusively on fixed, pre-calibrated camera rigs. Two datasets
motivate extending the evaluation framework to moving cameras. MultiCamRobolab~\cite{multicamrobolab}
introduces a real-world multi-camera dataset with ground-truth poses from a motion
capture system, targeting the setting of multiple freely moving cameras capturing a
shared dynamic scene. Extending the pipeline to this setting would require integrating
odometry data into the alignment stage, since the static mask derivation currently uses
pixel differences between frames and cannot distinguish camera-induced optical flow from
genuine scene motion. DyCheck~\cite{dycheck} provides a complementary evaluation
protocol for dynamic view synthesis from monocular video, introducing co-visibility
masked metrics and correspondence accuracy that overcome the multi-view signal leakage
present in existing benchmarks. Evaluating on both datasets would establish whether the
decoupled paradigm generalises beyond the controlled laboratory conditions of DexYCB
and Hi4D, and identify which components require adaptation for moving-camera or
single-camera settings.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This thesis addressed the problem of temporally consistent 4D reconstruction from
sparse multi-view video. The core challenge, that existing methods achieve either
geometric fidelity or temporal consistency but not both, is resolved by a decoupled
paradigm that keeps the two objectives separate and solves each with the appropriate
tool.

Three contributions support this conclusion. A unified benchmarking framework subjects
six reconstruction models to identical evaluation conditions across DexYCB and Hi4D,
producing the first systematic comparison of spatial, geometry-refinement, and native
4D architectures under controlled sparse-view conditions. A comparative alignment study
characterises three strategies for lifting per-frame reconstructions into a coherent 4D
representation, establishing that pairwise hierarchical alignment provides the best
balance of geometric stability and computational cost. Confidence-Weighted Temporal
Smoothing eliminates high-frequency jitter entirely and reduces mean jitter by
60--91\,\% across four spatial backbones, while preserving geometric fidelity, a
trade-off that native 4D architectures consistently fail to achieve.

The broader finding is that the decoupled paradigm is not a workaround but a
principled design choice. Geometric accuracy and temporal consistency conflict only
when temporal constraints are applied globally, penalising motion that is real.
Separating reconstruction from smoothing, and applying smoothing only where the physics
of the scene guarantee that consistency is valid, resolves the conflict entirely. The
results show that a strong per-frame spatial estimator with targeted post-processing
Pareto-dominates end-to-end joint architectures on the geometry--jitter trade-off at
every view count evaluated.

The remaining open problem is drift: the slow accumulation of pose estimation errors
that the low-pass filter cannot correct by design. Addressing drift through globally
coupled anchor-constrained optimisation, and extending the pipeline to close the
2-view to 4-view performance gap through reprojection-error refinement, are the natural
next steps. Both directions build directly on the framework established here and require
no additional training data, a property that made the current contribution practical
and that should guide the continuation of this work.
